He looked back at his screen. The graveyard folder was still open. Two hundred and eleven items. He'd never counted before — the number just lived there in Finder, updated automatically, a quiet ledger of everything he'd almost started. Some of the folders had sub-folders. One of them was called Research and contained eleven bookmarked articles he'd never read, about an industry he'd never entered, for a company he'd never named.

He scrolled. The files were sorted by date modified, newest first. Mood-Adaptive Workout App sat at the top, added four minutes ago. He kept scrolling. Past the lease negotiation tool. Past the carbon credit marketplace. Past the thing from last year that he still couldn't quite describe but had felt, at 11 PM on a Tuesday, like the most important idea he'd ever had.

He scrolled to the bottom. The oldest file was dated 2019. Kai stared at it for a long moment. He didn't remember putting it there. He didn't remember what was in it. 2019 was the year before everything changed. Before the pandemic, before the job, before he'd learned what it meant to have responsibilities that didn't bend to your midnight enthusiasm.

His finger hovered over the trackpad. He opened it. Then he closed it. He didn't read a word. He closed the laptop, leaned back in his seat at Gate B7, and listened to a toddler narrate the injustice of airport delays with more clarity than he'd managed in eight years. The boarding announcement came six minutes later. He got on the plane.
