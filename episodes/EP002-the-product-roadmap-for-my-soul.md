# The Aris Chronicles
## Episode 2: "The Product Roadmap for My Soul"

---

The meeting has been going for ninety-four minutes.

Kai Chen knows this because he started counting at the sixty-minute mark, when Marcus said "let's table that for now and circle back" for the second time, and he began tracking the table-and-circle-backs like a birdwatcher in a wetland. He is at five.

The meeting is a product roadmap review for Q2 at Meridian Technologies. There are twelve people in the room. A whiteboard with color-coded sticky notes. A laptop connected to a screen showing a Gantt chart that has been unchanged since November.

Fourteen months ago, Kai proposed a feature he called Smart Handoffs — an automated system for passing project context between team members when someone went on leave or changed roles. Not revolutionary. Practical. The kind of thing that would save every project manager at Meridian about three hours a week and prevent the specific kind of organizational amnesia that cost the company real money every quarter.

The feature is on the Gantt chart. In the column marked *Under Review*.

It has been in that column since the last review. And the review before that.

"The Smart Handoffs work item," Kai says, during a pause in the discussion. "Can we get an update on the review status?"

Marcus — his manager, a trim man in his late forties who has the bearing of someone who once believed very strongly in process — nods slowly. "Still working through the technical feasibility analysis with the platform team. Should have more clarity by end of quarter."

End of Q2 is eleven weeks away.

"Great," says Kai.

He makes a note in his laptop. Not about the meeting. About the meeting — but from the outside. He writes: *Smart Handoffs: 14 months, still "under review." Feature addresses a real problem. No technical blockers I've ever been told about. It simply doesn't have a champion above Marcus. That's the actual blocker.*

Then he opens the graveyard folder and adds a new item: `intelligent-meeting-summarizer.md`.

The meeting is generating its own content.

---

The core idea is this: AI that listens to a meeting and produces not a transcript but a *decision log*. What was decided. What was explicitly punted. What is still unresolved. Named owners for named actions, formatted for the human beings who didn't want to be in the meeting any more than necessary.

He wants it badly. He is in this meeting and wants a thing that doesn't exist yet. That is always the cleanest signal.

He spends the next twenty-two minutes (they have now been in the meeting for two hours and sixteen minutes) developing the concept in the new doc while nodding periodically at the discussion. He is not proud of this. He is also very good at it.

"Kai, any thoughts on the mobile priority stack?"

He looks up. Five faces.

"I think we're overcomplicating the prioritization framework," he says. "The users who are churning in mobile aren't leaving because of missing features — they're leaving because the onboarding takes eleven minutes. I'd fix that before adding anything."

There's a pause. Then Marcus writes something on the whiteboard and says, "Good point, let's put that in the parking lot."

A new sticky note goes up. Yellow. *Mobile onboarding — Kai.*

Parking lot.

---

After the meeting, Marcus catches him in the hallway.

"Can I get a minute?"

They walk to Marcus's office, which has a standing desk he has never actually stood at and a family photo that is slightly off-center on the wall, and Marcus closes the door.

"Your energy seems off lately," Marcus says. Not unkindly. He means it.

Kai looks at him for a moment. He thinks about the fourteen-month sticky note. He thinks about the parking lot. He thinks about the 212 items in a folder on his laptop.

"I'm fine," he says. "Just tired. The flight back from Chicago was late."

"We appreciate what you bring to the team," Marcus says. This is not the same as saying *I hear you.* Kai knows the difference. He doesn't say so.

"Thanks," he says. "I'll dial it in."

He goes back to his desk.

---

Devon finds him at 4:30, just before the unofficial end of the workday that nobody announces but everyone observes.

Devon is the engineering manager for Kai's product vertical. He is tall, precise in everything he does, and constitutionally allergic to hype. He is also, quietly, one of the people at Meridian who Kai respects without reservation.

"Have you seen that new AI stuff?" Devon says. He says it the way people say *have you tried this restaurant* — casually, like it's been on his mind since lunch.

"Which stuff?" Kai says.

"Not ChatGPT. The agentic stuff. Someone sent me a link." He's looking at his phone. "It's not a chatbot — it's more like a... contractor. You give it a project and it actually works through the steps. Calls tools, writes code, deploys things. I watched a demo video and it built a working CRUD app in about forty minutes."

Kai has heard adjacent things. He has the instinct of someone who has been burned by hype — the same instinct that protected him from cryptocurrency speculation and from the three months in 2022 when everyone on LinkedIn had an NFT project.

"Is it actually different or is it just a better demo?"

Devon shrugs. "I don't know yet. The demo was good."

"Send me the link."

Devon sends it. They talk for four more minutes about a staffing issue that is nobody's fault and everyone's problem, and then Devon goes home and Kai goes home and the day ends the way most of them do: accumulation without momentum.

---

That night, Kai opens the link Devon sent.

It's a twelve-minute YouTube video. He watches it on his couch with the TV on mute in the background.

The demo is: a person types a description of a web application they want. Not code — just English. *"I want a simple app where small teams can log daily standup notes and search them by keyword."* And then the agent — the AI — starts working. It writes code. It runs tests. It catches errors and fixes them. It deploys. Twelve minutes later, there's a link to a live application.

The person clicks around the app. It works.

Kai watches the video again.

The third time, he pauses it at the 8:23 mark, where the agent is mid-build, and he stares at the progress on the screen. Not the code — the *concept*. That a person with an idea and no engineering background could describe a thing they wanted and get, not a prototype, not a proof of concept, but a real working product.

He picks up his phone.

He types a search.

He finds the tool. He signs up for the free trial.

He opens a blank prompt.

He types: *Hello. What can you do?*

The response is long and capable-sounding and slightly formal. He reads it twice.

He types: *Can you summarize a meeting transcript and extract all decisions, unresolved items, and action items with named owners?*

It says yes. He pastes in the notes from today's roadmap review.

The output is ready in eleven seconds.

It is, objectively, a better artifact than anything the two-hour meeting produced.

He closes the laptop. He opens the TV. He watches something he won't remember.

Before he sleeps, he opens the laptop one more time. He goes to the graveyard folder. He opens `intelligent-meeting-summarizer.md`.

He writes, at the bottom: *It can do this. I just watched it do it.*

He saves the file.

He doesn't put it in the graveyard. He leaves it open on his desktop.
