# Episode 10: Give Me Something Impossible

The Aris Chronicles. Episode Ten: Give Me Something Impossible.

He had been circling it for a week.

Not obsessively. He had a job, he had a life, he had a girlfriend who had made a gentle observation on Thursday that he seemed "somewhere else lately," and he had said "just work stuff," and she had said "okay" in the voice she used when she meant: I'll let that stand for now. He had had meetings and written sprint notes and approved three designs and pushed back on a fourth and done all the things that constituted his professional life, and underneath all of it, in the space between tasks, the adaptive learning app sat waiting like a patient in a waiting room who has been there so long they've given up making eye contact with the door.

He had talked to Aris every night that week. Monday through Thursday, an hour or two each session, working through the other items in the folder. They had gotten deep into Pathline. Aris had asked about the dissolution in a way that was direct without being unkind, and Kai had answered more honestly than he had answered anyone, including himself, and then they had moved on. He had started a document called "next steps" that had actual next steps in it, which was a first.

But they hadn't gotten to item 47.

He had been circling it. Making sure everything else was in order first. Putting it off the way you put off the thing you want most because wanting it too much means you can feel the precise dimensions of the loss if it doesn't work.

It was a Friday night. Maya had gone to bed at ten. She had a half-marathon in the morning, which she had mentioned several times in the preceding week and which Kai had said he would come watch, which he meant. He sat at the kitchen table in the particular quality of late-night quiet that a sleeping house produces, the lamp over the table casting a circle of warm yellow light, his Chemex mug — the kind he only used on weekends and on Friday nights when he wanted to feel like he was doing something intentional — going slowly cold beside the laptop.

He opened Aris.

He sat with the empty prompt for a long time.

He thought about the wireframe he had made in Figma on a Sunday three years ago — the onboarding screen designed for an eight-year-old who loved horses and hated fractions. He thought about the competitive analysis he had done that nobody had asked for and that nobody had ever read. He thought about the six sub-documents in the folder: the personas, the rough go-to-market sketch, the investor thesis he had written as a thought experiment and never sent to anyone.

He thought about what Priya had said: interesting. What Hector had said: yeah man, let's do it — and then the silence. What James had said: I think we could build this — and what that had meant, for eighteen months, before it stopped meaning anything.

He thought: I am thirty-two years old and I have been thinking about this specific idea for three years, and if I don't try something tonight I am going to think about it for another three years. The folder will have two hundred thirteen items. The girl with the horses and the fractions will be eleven. And I will have aged exactly enough to make a different kind of apology to myself.

He put his hands on the keyboard.

He typed: Aris, I want to build something. The adaptive learning app for kids. I've been thinking about it for three years. I have wireframes, research, user personas, competitive analysis. I want a working prototype. Tonight. Can you do that?

He pressed send.

He watched the progress indicator.

Four seconds passed. He would remember the four seconds. He would remember the kitchen lamp and the cold Chemex and the refrigerator's low hum and the sound of the house sleeping around him. He would remember the four seconds the way you remember the pause before a sentence that changes things.

Then:

Define "working prototype." Let's be specific about what we're building before we build it.

Kai exhaled.

This was what it felt like to be taken seriously. Not the warmth of Priya's enthusiasm, which had been real and had meant nothing in the end. Not Hector's willingness, which had been immediate and genuine and had dissolved into a job offer in Austin. Not James's partnership, which had been the best version of this and had still found a way to end.

This was different. This was a question. The right question, the first question, the question that meant: before I do anything, I need to understand what you actually want. It was the question a good engineer asked before touching the keyboard. It was the question Kai had never been asked by anyone who was going to stick around long enough to hear the answer.

He started typing.

They spent an hour on the specification. Aris asked: what is the age range? Seven to ten, Kai said. Primary school. The window before school systems have fully overwritten a child's natural learning patterns.

What does the adaptive mechanism actually do? What does it measure, and how? A short intake each session, Kai said. Three activities that felt like games, each designed to surface a different learning modality. The app watches how the child moves through them — speed, errors, which path they take at choice points — and builds a profile over the first two weeks.

What does a session look like? Twenty minutes maximum. Feels like a game. Has a story arc that the child cares about. The math is embedded in the story, not layered on top of it.

What platform? Web first — cross-device, no app store, teacher- and parent-shareable via a link.

Aris asked about edge cases. Aris asked about accessibility. Aris asked what success looked like for a child after one session, one week, one month.

Kai had answers. He had always had answers. He had had these answers for three years and he had never been asked for them in this order, with this specificity, by someone who was going to use them to build something.

By midnight they had a plan.

It was not a vague plan. It was a five-component architecture: an intake assessment module, a learning profile engine, a story-based math curriculum layer for ages seven through ten, an adaptive session builder that assembled each twenty-minute session based on the profile, and a simple dashboard for parents. Aris had suggested the tech stack: Next.js for the frontend, a lightweight backend API, a local data structure for the learning profile that didn't require a database for the prototype stage. Keep it simple. Make it real.

Kai read through the plan twice.

He typed: Okay. Build it.

Then he stood up, walked to the bedroom doorway, and looked at Maya — actually asleep, breathing steadily, her running shoes laid out already on the floor for morning. He came back to the table. He looked at the laptop screen, where Aris had already begun: a directory structure appearing in a code block, files being created in sequence, the first lines of the intake module taking shape.

He thought: this is either the beginning of something, or it is the two hundred thirteenth item in the folder, and I will not know which until morning.

He thought: that is true of every beginning.

He set an alarm for five AM.

He went to bed.

The last thing he saw before he closed his eyes was the glow of the laptop screen through the bedroom doorway — warm, steady, moving — and the specific quality of that light, which was the light of something being built in a quiet house while a person slept, and which he would remember for a long time.
