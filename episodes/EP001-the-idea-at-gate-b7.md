# The Aris Chronicles
## Episode 1: "The Idea at Gate B7"

---

Gate B7 at O'Hare. 9:47 PM. Flight to San Francisco delayed until 11:15, which Kai Chen discovered by boarding the plane, sitting down, and then being asked to leave the plane.

He is now at a plastic chair near a Garrett Popcorn kiosk, his laptop open, a half-eaten bag of Chicago Mix on his thigh, and a quarterly review deck in a tab he has not opened once.

Instead, he is writing a document called `moodworkout_v1.md`.

The idea arrived somewhere over the boarding process — specifically during the moment when the gate agent said "folks, we're going to need everyone to deplane" and the collective groan of sixty-three people landed in his chest like a chord. He felt it: mood, movement, the specific physics of frustration becoming energy. What if a workout adapted to how you actually felt? Not what a fitness app's algorithm decided you *should* need. What you actually needed. Angry? Here's a heavy lift circuit. Anxious? Here's a long walk with a specific playlist. Numb? Here's the one that usually brings people back.

He is typing faster now. The doc has eight bullet points and a rough monetization section. He adds a note about user psychology, another about retention mechanics, a third about the specific problem with existing fitness apps (they assume you want to optimize; most people just want to survive the day).

It's good. He knows it's good. He's written enough of these to know the difference between ideas that arrive as fog and ideas that arrive as architecture — this one has weight, rooms, a floor plan.

He saves the file.

He opens the folder.

`_graveyard/`

It contains 211 items.

He drags `moodworkout_v1.md` inside. 212.

---

The folder is organized chronologically. The oldest item dates to 2019: a concept for a peer tutoring app that would pair college students with high schoolers in their same intended major. *Subject: Future You.* He remembers writing it. He was 25, riding a bus to his first real job, thinking about how badly he could have used a person like that at 17.

He doesn't open it. He closes the folder.

This is the ritual. Kai Chen, 32, senior product manager at Meridian Technologies, has been performing it for seven years. Idea arrives. Document gets made. Document joins the others. The folder does not shrink.

He is not sure why he keeps doing it. It's not optimism, exactly — he passed optimism somewhere around item 60 and landed somewhere murkier. More like... evidence collection. The folder is proof that he has been paying attention. That the world keeps presenting problems and he keeps noticing them. Whether anyone ever builds the solutions is, apparently, a separate question.

His phone buzzes. Slack. His manager, Marcus, sharing the Q2 roadmap deck "for reference" at 9:52 PM. Kai looks at the notification for three seconds, then puts his phone face-down on the seat next to him.

---

He buys a coffee from a kiosk that has no right charging six dollars for what it's serving. He drinks it standing up, looking at the departure board. His flight is still delayed. Outside the windows, a baggage cart is parked crookedly near a gate with no plane attached to it. A man in a reflective vest is leaning against it, looking at his phone.

Kai thinks: *what does that guy do when he gets home? Does he have a folder?*

Everyone has a version of the folder, he's decided. The drawer with the half-finished screenplay. The voicemail to yourself you sent during a drive and never transcribed. The napkin sketches in a shoebox under the bed. The ideas people announce at parties — *I've always thought someone should make a thing that* — and then let dissolve into the ambient noise of the evening.

He's just more systematic about his version.

He goes back to the laptop. The quarterly deck is still unopened. He opens it now and reads the first three slides. He closes it.

He opens the graveyard folder.

Item 47. He finds it without searching — he knows roughly where it lives in the chronology: three years ago, late fall. A concept for an adaptive learning app for kids. Personalized, playful, built around the way children actually absorb information rather than the way curricula assume they do. He spent a week on this one. There are six sub-documents inside the folder: competitive analysis, user persona sketches, a rough wireframe he'd drawn in Figma on a weekend afternoon, a failed conversation with a developer friend who said he was "interested" and then stopped responding.

The app doesn't exist. The Figma file is still somewhere in his cloud storage. The developer friend is now at Google and they exchange birthday messages once a year.

He reads through the core doc. It holds up. Better than he expected. The ideas are good.

He closes the laptop.

The departure board updates. 11:15 is now 11:45.

He buys another coffee.

---

At 11:58 PM, boarding begins. Kai files down the jetway behind a woman with a child on her hip and a man in a suit who is somehow still on a phone call. He finds his seat — window, 22A, which he paid eleven dollars to choose — and he opens the laptop again before the wheels are up.

He opens the graveyard folder.

He scrolls to the bottom. 212 items, the newest at the top.

He scrolls to item 47 again. The adaptive learning app. He opens it this time.

He reads the first paragraph:

*"There is no good reason that every 8-year-old in the country is learning math the same way. We have the data. We have the tools. What we don't have is the product that actually builds around the child instead of around the classroom."*

He wrote that at 26. On a Sunday. After watching his niece spend forty minutes staring at a worksheet that had no interest in her whatsoever.

He stares at the screen.

The plane lifts.

Outside the window: Chicago spreads below, orange and gridded, a circuit board the size of a city.

He opens a new tab. He is going to look something up. Something Devon mentioned last week — not ChatGPT, he'd said. *The agentic stuff.* The thing that could actually do tasks, not just answer questions.

He types into the search bar.

He will not find what he's looking for tonight. But he's pointing in the right direction.

The plane banks west.
