# The Aris Chronicles — Episode 1: The Idea at Gate B7

---

The flight was delayed forty-seven minutes, which meant Kai Chen had forty-seven minutes to ruin himself.

He knew this about airports. They were incubators for bad decisions — the kind that felt like insight. Something about the liminal quality of terminals, the recycled air and the ambient roar of people going somewhere else, loosened the part of his brain that was supposed to maintain realistic expectations. He'd read somewhere that high-altitude pressure changes affected cognition even on the ground. He'd never verified this. He had, however, added it to a note titled "Altitude Cognition — startup angle??" two years ago. That note lived in the graveyard folder. Item 194.

He had a window seat at Gate B7. His laptop was open. His Slack had forty-two unread messages, most of them about a roadmap review happening in eleven days that he was supposed to be preparing for. The meeting agenda — sent by his manager, Elena, with the subject line "Prep This Weekend Please (No Pressure)" — sat in a browser tab, patient and accusatory.

He had not opened it.

Instead, he'd opened a new document.

The cursor blinked. He typed: Mood-Adaptive Workout App.

Kai sat back and looked at it the way you look at a scratch-off ticket before you scratch it — with the full knowledge that it probably isn't anything, but not quite ready to confirm that yet. A workout app that read your energy. Integrated with your calendar, your sleep data, your screen time. Knew when you were burned out. Knew when you were wired. Built the session around the person you were today, not some ideal version of you that exercised at 6 AM and ate clean and didn't spend forty-five minutes reading about altitude cognition at airports.

It was, he admitted, a real problem. He had a Whoop. He had a Garmin. Neither of them cared that he was exhausted on Tuesdays specifically because of standing Monday syncs that always ran long. They just noted his elevated resting heart rate with the cheerful indifference of machines.

He wrote three more lines. A feature list. The word "adaptive" appeared four times.

Then he stopped.

He'd been here before. Not Gate B7, specifically — though statistically, given his flight schedule, probably there too — but here, in this exact psychological neighborhood. The place where the idea felt like momentum. Where naming a thing felt like building it. He knew this neighborhood well. He'd lived in it for eight years.

Kai saved the document. Opened Finder. Navigated to the folder he kept on his desktop, labeled with an underscore so it sorted to the top: _graveyard.

He dragged the file in.

The folder contained 211 items.

He sat with that number for a moment. Two hundred and eleven ideas. Some of them were half-sentences, the kind you jot at 2 AM when your brain is doing the thing where it mistakes exhaustion for creativity. Some were actual decks — forty, fifty slides, with TAM analysis and competitive landscapes and a slide titled "Why Now" that he'd filled in with varying degrees of conviction. One of them had a working prototype. He didn't let himself think about that one too long.

His phone buzzed. Elena, in Slack: "Hey Kai — making sure you got the agenda. Also the Loom I sent Tuesday has some context for the Q2 section."

He typed back: "On it." Two words, which was technically true in the sense that he was aware the agenda existed.

A gate agent announced something incomprehensible over the intercom. A toddler three rows of seats away expressed a strong opinion about this. The man next to Kai smelled faintly of airport Auntie Anne's pretzels, which Kai now desperately wanted.

He looked back at his screen. The _graveyard folder was still open.

Two hundred and eleven items. He'd never counted before — the number just lived there in Finder, updated automatically, a quiet ledger of everything he'd almost started. Some of the folders had sub-folders. One of them was called "Research" and contained eleven bookmarked articles he'd never read, about an industry he'd never entered, for a company he'd never named.

He scrolled. The files were sorted by date modified, newest first. Mood-Adaptive Workout App sat at the top, added four minutes ago. He kept scrolling. Past the AI-powered lease negotiation tool. Past the carbon credit marketplace for small businesses. Past the thing from last year that he still couldn't quite describe but had felt, at 11 PM on a Tuesday, like the most important idea he'd ever had.

He scrolled to the bottom.

The oldest file was dated 2019.

Kai stared at it for a long moment. He didn't remember putting it there. He didn't remember what was in it. The filename was just a string of numbers — a timestamp, like he'd exported it from somewhere and never renamed it. 2019 was the year before everything changed. Before the pandemic, before the job, before he'd learned what it meant to have responsibilities that didn't bend to your midnight enthusiasm.

His finger hovered over the trackpad.

He opened it.

Then he closed it.

He didn't read a word.

He closed the laptop, leaned back in his seat at Gate B7, and listened to a toddler narrate the injustice of airport delays with more clarity than he'd managed in eight years.

The boarding announcement came six minutes later.

He got on the plane.
