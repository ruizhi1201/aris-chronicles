# The Aris Chronicles
## Episode 3: "211 Funerals"

---

It's a Saturday in October and Kai Chen is sitting at the kitchen table with his coffee going cold beside him and the graveyard folder open for the first time in months.

He does this occasionally. Not often — the same way you might visit a place that used to mean something to you, not because you're stuck there but because you want to confirm that you've moved on and find that you haven't entirely. He is thirty-two years old. He opens the folder the way people open old journals. With a mix of tenderness and shame.

He starts at the top, which is always the most recent: the items he's added in the last year. `moodworkout_v1.md`, from the O'Hare delay. A tool for tracking home maintenance tasks that he conceived while on hold with a plumber. A subscription service for locally-sourced pantry staples (too many dependencies, abandoned before the document was done). A consumer app for medication reminders with intelligent refill prediction. A B2B platform for freelance contractors to share reference templates and proposal frameworks.

He opens each one in sequence, reads the first few paragraphs, and closes them. He is not evaluating these. He is remembering who he was when he wrote them. The plumber hold: he'd been sitting on his kitchen floor for forty minutes, staring at a leaking pipe, and the product idea had been the only productive thing he could do with his brain.

He keeps scrolling.

---

The older ones are stranger to him now. 2021: a mental health journaling tool with a check-in system. 2020: a community app for apartment buildings (he was in lockdown, everyone was lonely, it seemed obvious). 2019: *Subject: Future You*, the peer tutoring app. He opens that one. He is surprised, again, that it's good. The writing is less polished — he was younger, rougher — but the core insight holds. The problem is real. Nobody solved it for him.

He keeps scrolling.

He is looking, he realizes, for pattern. The things he built — built in his head, built in documents, never built in the world — have a signature. They are all about friction. The specific friction between what a person needs and what the available systems provide. He notices this problem everywhere. On the airplane, in the meeting, on the kitchen floor waiting for a plumber. He is a friction-detector. He always has been.

The tragedy, if it is one, is that detecting friction isn't the same as removing it.

---

Item 117 is a note document from 2021, not a product concept. He'd titled it `why does this keep happening.md`. He opens it.

*"I have now tried to find a technical co-founder six times. I've done the coffee meetings, the intro emails, the accelerator pitch events, the Twitter DMs. Here's what I've learned: technical people are not scarce. People who will commit to a project that doesn't yet pay them — who will stay committed when a better opportunity appears — are extremely scarce. I'm not being cynical. I'm trying to be precise. The gap isn't technical skill. The gap is commitment under uncertainty. Everyone is busy. Everyone has options. An idea that isn't real yet is not competitive with an idea that already has a salary attached to it."*

He doesn't remember writing this, but he recognizes himself in it. The tone is tired. It reads like a field report from a war he'd been losing for years.

He thinks about Priya. He thinks about Hector. He thinks — less willingly, more persistently — about James.

Priya was first: a CS graduate student he met at a startup event. Smart, interested, enthusiastic about the education app for six weeks. Then FAANG came calling. She sent him a gracious email. He congratulated her. That was three years ago. He still sees her posts on LinkedIn. She is doing well.

Hector was the one that almost made it. Six months of actual work — the real kind, with meetings and a shared Notion and a domain purchased in both their names. A product called Pathfinder, a career navigation tool for people in their late twenties who'd taken the wrong turn somewhere. They'd had twelve beta users. Real people who checked in every week and said things like *I don't know what I'd do without this.*

Then Hector got an offer in Austin.

Then the equity conversation got complicated.

Then Hector's messages started coming slower, and slower, and then they stopped.

---

The James story is the one Kai doesn't open.

It's in here — he knows the approximate location in the chronology — and he does not open it today. Not this Saturday. Some Saturdays.

He was going to build something real with James. They did, for a while. Eighteen months. An actual company, incorporated, two paying customers. Pathline. Career navigation, again — Kai kept coming back to this problem. He was a product manager who'd watched a hundred people make wrong turns for institutional rather than personal reasons, and he cared about it.

James wanted to pivot into corporate outplacement, where the money was. Kai thought that killed the whole point. The argument took three weeks and ended with a formal dissolution and a legal document signed by two people who'd been drinking coffee in each other's apartments for a year and a half.

The company died on a Tuesday.

One of the beta users emailed him the following spring, asking when the product was coming back. Kai read the email twice and then left it unanswered in his inbox, where it lived for eleven months before he archived it.

He still thinks about that email sometimes.

---

He is at item 47 before he realizes it.

The adaptive learning app. He has been circling it.

He opens the folder. There are sub-documents: the wireframe notes, the competitive analysis he spent a weekend on, the user personas he'd named and described in enough detail that they'd started to feel like real children. *Maya (not his girlfriend — he named her before he met his girlfriend, which is a coincidence that became awkward to explain). Eight years old. Loves horses. Hates fractions. Not because she's not smart — because nobody has explained fractions in terms of horses yet.*

He built these kids. He has never built the thing for them.

He hears the front door.

Maya — his actual Maya — comes in with groceries and the particular energy of someone who has been out in the world and found it full of things to carry.

"What are you doing?" she asks, not critically. Curious.

"Just cleaning up some files," he says.

This is not true. He is not cleaning. He has not deleted anything. He has not reorganized anything. He has been sitting with the dead for two hours and calling it housekeeping.

Maya puts the groceries down and comes to look at the screen. She sees a folder structure, some documents, a wireframe screenshot open in another tab — the adaptive learning app's onboarding flow, which he'd sketched in Figma on a Sunday three years ago and never shown to anyone.

"For work?" she asks.

"Old stuff," he says. "Just looking."

She kisses the top of his head and goes to put away the groceries. She does not ask more questions. This is love, in one form: the mercy of not always requiring explanation.

---

He sits there for another twenty minutes after she's moved to the other room.

He stares at the wireframe. Eight-year-old Maya with her fractions and her horses. The onboarding screen he'd designed for her, imagining how she would hold the phone, how many words she could read before her attention migrated somewhere else, what would make her laugh.

He closes Figma.

He opens his laptop and navigates back to the AI tool he'd tried two nights ago. The one from Devon's link. He'd tested it on a meeting transcript and been impressed and then closed it and gone to bed and not thought about it seriously since.

He opens a new prompt.

He types: *I have an idea for an adaptive learning app for children. I've been thinking about it for three years. What would you need to know to help me build it?*

The response comes in about five seconds. It is a list of questions — precise, useful, organized into categories: user, market, technical dependencies, success metrics.

He reads the questions.

They are exactly the right questions.

He looks up from the screen. Through the kitchen doorway he can see Maya putting apples in a bowl, unhurried, in the good Saturday light.

He looks back at the screen.

He starts answering the questions.
